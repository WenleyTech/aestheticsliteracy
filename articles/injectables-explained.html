Injectables Explained: What They Are and How They Work

A science-based guide to neuromodulators and dermal fillers

Injectable treatments are among the most common non-surgical aesthetic procedures worldwide. While often grouped together, they fall into two very different scientific categories: neuromodulators and dermal fillers.

1. Neuromodulators (Often Called “Botox”)

Neuromodulators use a purified protein derived from botulinum toxin type A. When injected into specific facial muscles, they temporarily reduce muscle movement.

How They Work

  • Block nerve signals that tell muscles to contract
  • Relax overactive facial muscles
  • Soften dynamic wrinkles (lines caused by movement)

Common Treatment Areas

  • Forehead lines
  • Frown lines between the brows
  • Crow’s feet around the eyes

Important: Neuromodulators do not “fill” wrinkles. They reduce muscle activity that causes them.

2. Dermal Fillers

Dermal fillers are gel-like substances injected beneath the skin to restore lost volume, enhance contours, or smooth static wrinkles.

How They Work

  • Add volume beneath the skin
  • Support facial structure
  • Improve skin hydration (some fillers attract water)

Main Types of Fillers

  • Hyaluronic Acid (HA) – Most common; reversible
  • Calcium Hydroxylapatite – Thicker, structural support
  • Poly-L-lactic Acid – Stimulates collagen production

Common Treatment Areas

  • Lips
  • Cheeks
  • Nasolabial folds (smile lines)
  • Jawline and chin

How Long Do Results Last?

  • Neuromodulators: 3–4 months on average
  • Fillers: 6 months to 2+ years depending on type and area

Safety and Provider Skill

Injectables are medical procedures and should be performed by qualified, licensed professionals. Outcomes depend heavily on:

  • Anatomy knowledge
  • Injection technique
  • Product selection

Understanding the science behind these treatments helps patients ask better questions and make informed decisions.
