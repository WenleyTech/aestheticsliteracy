Injectables Explained: What They Are and How They Work

A science-based guide to neuromodulators and dermal fillers—what they do, what they don’t do, and how to evaluate provider skill.

Category: Injectables Reading time: ~6–8 min Updated: 2026

On this page

1) What “Injectables” Means

Injectable treatments are among the most common non-surgical aesthetic procedures worldwide. People often group them together, but they fall into two different categories: neuromodulators and dermal fillers.

Key takeaway

  • Neuromodulators relax targeted muscles to soften movement-based lines.
  • Fillers add volume or structure under the skin.
  • The best outcomes come from anatomy knowledge + conservative planning.

2) Neuromodulators (Often Called “Botox”)

Neuromodulators use a purified protein that temporarily reduces muscle movement. This helps soften dynamic wrinkles (lines caused by movement).

How they work

  • Block nerve signals that tell muscles to contract
  • Relax overactive facial muscles
  • Soften dynamic lines over time

Common treatment areas

  • Forehead lines
  • Frown lines between the brows
  • Crow’s feet
Important: Neuromodulators do not “fill” wrinkles. They reduce the muscle activity that causes them.

3) Dermal Fillers

Dermal fillers are gel-like substances injected beneath the skin to restore volume, enhance contours, or support facial structure.

How they work

  • Add volume beneath the skin
  • Support facial structure
  • Some attract water and improve hydration

Main types (high-level)

  • Hyaluronic Acid (HA) — common; reversible
  • Calcium Hydroxylapatite — thicker; structural support
  • Poly-L-lactic Acid — stimulates collagen over time

4) How Long Results Last

  • Neuromodulators: often ~3–4 months (varies)
  • Fillers: depends on product + area; can range widely

5) Safety + Provider Skill

Injectables are medical procedures. Outcomes depend heavily on provider training, facial anatomy knowledge, product selection, and dosing strategy.

Rule of thumb: A safe injector welcomes questions, explains tradeoffs clearly, and is conservative when unsure.

6) Questions to Ask at Your Consult

  • What license do you hold, and what training do you have for this procedure?
  • How do you decide dosing / placement for my face?
  • What are the most common side effects, and what rare risks should I understand?
  • What is the plan if I dislike the result?
